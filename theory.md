## Lesson 1 — The 6 Main Areas of Music

Everyone wants to play a musical instrument at some point, but few stick with it. It is
usually not a lack of TALENT or PRACTICE — it is that they never got the SATISFACTION
they expected, and over time they lost interest.

A lot of playing well is simply **INFORMATION**: understanding what you are doing.
Music is often taught in the WRONG ORDER, so people never see how it all fits together.
Music is actually a very simple subject to understand.

**MUSIC IS A LANGUAGE.** Like any language, music has an ALPHABET. You select units from
that alphabet and form them into PATTERNS. These patterns have MEANING, are very much like
"words", and have SPELLINGS.

**YOUR GUITAR IS A TYPEWRITER** — a "dumb machine". When you move your fingers on the neck
you are "TYPING" these patterns. Learning shapes without knowing what they mean is a dead
end. You don't play guitar with your fingers — you play it with your MIND. You must
understand the LANGUAGE of music before you can fully understand the guitar.

### What 2 things must you do to become a REAL PLAYER?

1. **GET TO THE END OF THE SONG.** Don't confuse playing RIFFS (little bits) with playing
   whole SONGS. Real artists produce complete works.
2. **STOP AND ASK YOURSELF "HOW DID I DO?"** To progress you need a system to analyze your
   deficiencies. Record yourself and listen as an impartial listener. But to judge how you
   did, you first have to know what music _is_.

There are only **6 things going on in music**. If you understand these 6 concepts, you can
ask yourself 6 checklist questions after playing. In other words:

### MUSIC IS A LANGUAGE WITH 6 PARTS OF SPEECH — The 6 Main Areas of Music

| Area          | What it covers                                                                                                                         | Checklist question                |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| **Pitch**     | High/Low NOTES · Frequency · ~50 "words" (PATTERNS: scales, chords, melody, harmony, intervals, modes, progressions, arpeggios, riffs) | Did I play the right notes?       |
| **Rhythm**    | Beat · Tempo · Timing · Pulse                                                                                                          | Did I keep with the beat?         |
| **Timbre**    | Tone · Sound Quality · Instruments · EQ · Effects (e.g. Distortion)                                                                    | How did I SOUND?                  |
| **Dynamics**  | Volume · Loudness · Amplitude · Balance · up and down through the song · size of room                                                  | Did I play at the right volume?   |
| **Technique** | The physical part · Finger movement · How to hold the instrument                                                                       | Did I cooperate with the machine? |
| **Notation**  | Reading written music                                                                                                                  | How is my Reading coming?         |

To be a REAL PLAYER: **play the whole song** and be able to **answer YES to these 6
questions**.

## Lesson 2 — 4 Ways to Know Your Pitch Patterns

PITCH is the largest area of music. It is all about HIGH and LOW — specifically, high and
low **NOTES**, and the PATTERNS you arrange those notes into (like CHORDS and SCALES). The
scientific word for pitch is **FREQUENCY**. Everything making noise is VIBRATING and
sending out SOUND WAVES; the frequency is how far apart the waves are.

- Low Frequency = low pitch. High Frequency = high pitch.

Your guitar is a **SOUND WAVE GENERATOR**. A string stretched between two points vibrates
when plucked. The SPEED at which it vibrates defines the frequency (slow = waves far apart
= low pitch; fast = waves close together = high pitch).

**PITCH = FREQUENCY = SPEED OF VIBRATION OF THE STRING.**

We control the string's vibrating speed using **3 factors**:

1. **The MASS of the string** — thicker/more massive strings vibrate slower (that is why
   the strings are different thicknesses).
2. **The TENSION of the string** — tightening or loosening changes the speed. This is what
   TUNING is, and also how you BEND notes.
3. **The LENGTH of the string** — you shorten the vibrating length by pressing FRETS.

### How many musical notes are there? — The answer is 12

Most people are taught there are only 7 (A–G), but that is not accurate. The five "black
keys" (sharps/flats) are just as real as the seven "white keys". The guitar plays these
same **12 notes**, which then repeat in higher and lower **OCTAVES**.

You have to learn to form about **50 patterns** out of this 12-note alphabet:
**13 intervals, 14 scales, 20 chords.** Think of these as "words" with "spellings" made
from the 12 notes.

### The Musical Alphabet (memorize this first)

```
A  A#/Bb  B  C  C#/Db  D  D#/Eb  E  F  F#/Gb  G  G#/Ab  (A ...)
```

- **SHARP (#)** = HIGHER in pitch. **FLAT (b)** = LOWER in pitch. A# and Bb are two names
  for the same note.
- A **HALF STEP** is the interval from one note to the very next (e.g. E to F).
- A **WHOLE STEP** is two half steps.
- An **OCTAVE** is from one note to the next note of the same name (12 half steps).
- **B–C** and **E–F** have **no black key between them** — there is no sharp/flat there
  (they are a half step apart).

### 4 WAYS TO KNOW EACH PITCH PATTERN

You must learn each of the ~50 patterns in 4 different "faces":

| You have to…              | Which means knowing the pattern as… | World                                                  |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **THINK** in the language | **CONCEPTS**                        | Music Theory (memorize like spelling/grammar)          |
| **READ** in the language  | **NOTATION**                        | Standard notation, Tablature, Chord/Scale finger boxes |
| **HEAR** in the language  | **REAL SOUND**                      | Ear Training (recognize & sing all 50 patterns)        |
| **SPEAK** in the language | **FINGER PATTERNS**                 | Technique (scale/chord finger charts)                  |

## Lesson 3 — The "Dumb Machine"

Now that you know the 12-note alphabet, how do you "type" it on the guitar?

The 6 strings are tuned from LOW to HIGH: **E – A – D – G – B – E**. On a piano keyboard,
the notes your open strings are tuned to always have **4 keys in between them** — _except_
G and B, which have only 3.

**Why?** BECAUSE YOU HAVE 4 FINGERS. To play all 12 notes in sequence (a **CHROMATIC
SCALE**), start on the open LOW E string and play the first 4 frets with your 4 fingers;
after your pinky, move to the next open string, and so on. The G and B strings are tuned
only 3 notes apart because it makes more chord fingerings possible (playing the pinky note
on the G string would just repeat the open B).

- From open LOW E to the same point one octave up is 13 notes (**1st OCTAVE**); another 13
  gets you to the open HIGH E (**2nd OCTAVE**). Open LOW E to open HIGH E is **2 octaves**;
  the guitar's total range is about **4 octaves**.

**Tuning:** buy an electronic **CHROMATIC TUNER** (not a "guitar tuner", which won't handle
flat/altered tunings). Learning to tune by ear can come later.

### The 1st Rule of the Guitar: 4 FINGERS = 4 FRETS

The main physical skill is controlling 4 consecutive frets with your 4 fingers, coordinated
with alternate picking. A good test of control: **be able to touch all 4 frets at once.**

But it actually takes **5 FRETS** to control all 12 notes:

- At the **bottom of the neck**, the open string acts as that 5th fret.
- **Further up the neck** you must learn **FINGER SHIFTS** — controlling 2 consecutive
  frets with 1 finger.

**INDEX SHIFTS and PINKY SHIFTS** (from the fretboard diagrams):

- Going **UP** the scale: walk up until you reach your pinky, then reach an extra fret with
  your **INDEX** finger on the next string and "scoot" it back onto the fret it would
  normally be on, then continue. (**Index shift.**)
- Coming **DOWN** the scale: start with your pinky on the high E string, walk down to your
  index, then reach an extra fret with your **PINKY** on the next lower string and scoot it
  across. (**Pinky shift.**)
- **No shift is required between the G and B strings**, because they are tuned one note
  closer together.

If you can play a CHROMATIC SCALE anywhere on the neck, you can do anything in music — you
know where every note is.

## Lesson 4 — Basic Stuff About Chords

**CHORDS are multiple notes played SIMULTANEOUSLY.** There are about 50 types of chords in
Western music, and they all express different "MOODS" — the more chord types you can play,
the more emotions you can express. Common types: **Major, minor, 6, 7, 9, 11, 13, aug,
dim, sus.**

Like all pitch patterns, chords have "spellings", and fingering them is "typing" them.

- **There are 12 of each type of chord** (one per key), because there are 12 notes.
- **There are 5 basic finger FORMS** (the **E, A, D, G, C forms**). All 12 chords can be
  played using these 5 forms as movable/BAR chords: the bar finger replaces the open
  strings as you move up the neck.
- So **there are 5 ways to play any chord.** The 5 ways sound SIMILAR (same letter notes)
  but somewhat DIFFERENT (notes in a different order or octave).

### All chords have a ROOT NOTE

The **ROOT** is the lowest note in the chord that has the same name as the chord itself.
You must always know where the root is — it is the "launching pad".

On a chord finger chart:

- **Ⓡ** marks a root that is an **open string**; **●(circled/filled)** marks a root played
  by a finger.
- Chords whose root is not in the bass are called **SLASH CHORDS** (covered later).
- **X** over a string means don't play it (either skip or mute it).

**Diagram examples (E Form and moving it up):**

- **E Major in the E Form** is spelled E, G#, B (letters may repeat/reorder). The next
  higher major chord is F major, spelled F, A, C — there is no open "F Form", so one way to
  play F is to raise every note of the E Major shape up one fret (a bar chord). Raise it
  another fret and it becomes F# major, and so on up the neck — that is how you get all 12
  major chords from one shape.
- The same F Major can also be played as **F in the A Form, D Form, G Form, and C Form** —
  five different regions of the neck, each with the root on a different string.

### The Chord Chart (grid of forms × types)

A master grid shows the finger positions for each chord TYPE in each FORM. Rows are the
forms **E, A, D, G, C**; columns are common types with their "moods":

| Type          | Mood        |
| ------------- | ----------- |
| **Major**     | happy       |
| **Minor**     | sad         |
| **7**         | bluesey     |
| **Min 7**     | sad bluesey |
| **Maj 7**     | mellow      |
| **Power (5)** | severe      |

Numbers on the boxes indicate the most common fingerings (1=index … 4=pinky); Ⓡ marks the
root; open circles ○ mark optional added notes; X means mute the string if the alternate
note isn't used.

## Lesson 5 — More About Chords

### Chord names have 3 parts

**ROOT NOTE + TYPE of chord + FINGER FORM to play it in.** (You must often figure out the
form yourself.) Example: **Bb · Maj 7 · A Form.**

### How to play a chord from its name — WORK BACKWARDS

Read the name back to front:

1. Start with the **FORM** (if given).
2. Then find the **TYPE** of chord in that form (its basic finger shape).
3. Then turn it into a **BAR CHORD** and move it up the neck until the ROOT NOTE lands on
   the correct fret/string.

**Worked example — "Bb Maj7 in the A Form":** it is in the A Form (that row), and it is a
Maj7 (that column) → that is the basic shape. In the A Form the root is on the A string, so
the open version is A Maj7; to make it Bb Maj7 you bar it and move everything up one fret so
the root becomes Bb.

### The reverse skill — name a chord from its finger shape

Also WORK BACKWARDS:

1. What **FORM** is it? (Look at where the root note is. Root on the low E string → E Form
   or G Form. If the root is on the same fret as the bar, it started as an open string → E
   Form.)
2. What **TYPE** is it? (Picture the bar as the open strings and read the shape.)
3. Then find the actual root's fret to get the letter name.

**Worked examples:** a shape that looks like a 7 chord in the E Form with the root on the
open low E string is **E7 in the E Form**. Bar that same shape and raise it 3 frets so the
root moves to the 3rd fret of the low E (a G note) → **G7 in the E Form**.

Developing this skill lets you learn songs by **watching** others play — live or on video —
by reading where they put their fingers.

## Lesson 6 — Picking, Strumming and Practicing

Chords are pitch patterns and must be known 4 ways (THINK, READ, HEAR, SPEAK). Now you must
master actually PLAYING them so the guitar can SPEAK. This requires PRACTICE.

**The sad truth:** PLAYING chords is the EASY part. **SWITCHING from chord to chord with the
BEAT is the problem.** A very useful practice method:

- **PRACTICE CHORDS IN PAIRS** — switching is what you must work on.
- **BACK AND FORTH EVERY 4 BEATS** — ~80% of music is counted in even numbers like 2 and 4.
- **NON-STOP FOR 5 MINUTES** — strength and speed come from constant repetition.
- **WITH DRUMS** — all music has rhythm; practicing without a drum machine wastes your time.
- **SLOW as you need** — set a realistic tempo, then gradually increase it.
- **THINK AHEAD** — force your mind to the next chord before you get there, or you fall behind.
- **LISTEN to yourself** — chords must come out CLEAN, without muted or buzzing notes.

**Get a mechanical TIME KEEPER** (a drum machine beats a metronome — get one with good
sound and ~100 rhythms).

### Strumming — note durations (counting in 4/4)

Your strumming hand moves back and forth like a pendulum. The time between strokes is
expressed with rhythm-notation values:

| Note               | Lasts    | How to strum                                            |
| ------------------ | -------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **WHOLE note**     | 4 beats  | Strum DOWN on beat 1, let it ring for the whole measure |
| **HALF note**      | 2 beats  | Strum DOWN on beats 1 and 3                             |
| **QUARTER note**   | 1 beat   | 4 DOWN strums, one per beat                             |
| **EIGHTH note**    | 1/2 beat | DOWN/UP strokes, count "1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +"                |
| **SIXTEENTH note** | 1/4 beat | double-time ups and downs, "1 e + a 2 e + a …"          |

### Using strumming rates to switch faster

A chart (○ = strum, ✱ = "bail" / lift, dotted line = the time you have to form the 2nd
chord) shows that as you strum more often (1 down → 2 down → 4 down → down/up → double
time), you cut the time you have to switch chords in half each time. Keep challenging
yourself to switch faster.

### Strumming patterns

Songs would sound monotonous if strumming were perfectly regular, so we mix durations. Even
though the hand keeps going up and down, you **don't hit the strings on every stroke** — a
missed stroke lets the previous note ring longer. Every song has its own strumming pattern.

- **D = contact Down stroke, U = contact Up stroke, ● = missed stroke.**
- Common example counted "1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +": **D ● D U ● U D U** (hand moving at the eighth-note rate).

Later you add bass notes, accents, "pick clicks", and FINGER STYLE playing (each finger acts
as a separate pick).

## Lesson 7 — Music by the Numbers

There are about **50 basic pitch patterns**, but each can be played in any KEY. In any song,
one of the 12 notes acts as the "anchor" — the **NUCLEUS NOTE**. Once you identify it, you
know what KEY you're in. Since there are 12 notes, there are 12 keys.

If all 50 patterns could be played in all 12 keys, that's 600 things to memorize — but it's
not that bad. These patterns are **SPATIAL RELATIONSHIPS** between notes that can be
TRANSPOSED into any key. So instead of memorizing 12 letter-variations of each pattern, we
use an **ALTERNATIVE naming system** that treats music as if there were only ONE key. It is
called **THE MUSICAL NUMBER SYSTEM** (this is where C7 chords, 1-4-5 progressions, major
3rds, perfect 5ths come from). It makes memorizing spellings ~11 times easier.

### To what do the numbers correspond? — a MAJOR DIATONIC SCALE

The numbers number the notes in a **Major Diatonic Scale** — the "Do Re Mi" scale:

```
DO  RE  MI  FA  SO  LA  TI  DO
 1   2   3   4   5   6   7   8
```

A **SCALE** is a sequence of notes separated by **specific INTERVALS**. **INTERVALS** are
the pitch "distances" between notes. It isn't so much _what_ notes you play as _how far
apart_ they are spaced.

### The Major Scale interval pattern

A **MAJOR SCALE** goes **Whole – Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Whole – Half** (mostly whole
steps, except between **3 & 4** and **7 & 8**).

A **CHROMATIC SCALE** is a sequence of **13 notes all separated by HALF steps**.

The in-between notes are named by sharping/flatting the numbers, just like letters:

```
1   #1/b2   2   #2/b3   3   4   #4/b5   5   #5/b6   6   #6/b7   7   8
```

Numbers like **9, 10, 11, 13** are notes up in a SECOND OCTAVE (8=1, 9=2, 10=3, 11=4, …),
continuing the same major-scale pattern.

### Important pitch patterns expressed as numbers

| Pattern                           | Numbers                          |
| --------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Major chord**                   | 1 – 3 – 5                        |
| **Minor chord**                   | 1 – b3 – 5                       |
| **7 chord**                       | 1 – 3 – 5 – b7                   |
| **Power chord**                   | 1 – 5 – 8                        |
| **Natural minor scale**           | 1 – 2 – b3 – 4 – 5 – b6 – b7 – 8 |
| **Pentatonic scale**              | 1 – 2 – 3 – 5 – 6 – 8            |
| **Minor pentatonic**              | 1 – b3 – 4 – 5 – b7 – 8          |
| **Most common chord progression** | 1 – 4 – 5                        |

The **Major Diatonic Scale is the central pitch pattern in Western music** — every other
pattern is measured against it. There are many fingerings for the major scale on the neck;
the finger pattern directly reflects the W-W-H-W-W-W-H interval pattern and can be moved to
play the scale in any of the 12 keys. (Fretboard diagrams show 3 easy fingerings, with the
root marked as "Do"; two have no finger shifts.)

## Lesson 8 — Caveman Music Theory

**Where did the 12-note system come from?** Pitch is a CONTINUUM — infinitely many pitches
infinitely close together. Why did humans "chop it up" into 12-note octaves?

**HARMONICS.** Cavemen noticed that certain spots along a string produce very loud, clear
overtones ("harmonics") at precise MATHEMATICAL divisions of the string. Our whole system of
pitch is based on them. **The loudest harmonics occur directly over the 12th, 7th and 5th
frets.**

### What 3 numbers/notes are produced harmonically on any string?

**1 · 5 · 8**, from the 12th-fret, 7th-fret and 5th-fret harmonics respectively.

The intervals separating them:

- from **1 to 5** = a **PERFECT 5th**
- from **5 to 8** = a **PERFECT 4th**
- from **1 to 8** = a **PERFECT OCTAVE**

This is "the entire history of music": these 3 notes hold the whole language together and
are present in almost every moment of music. The OCTAVE is a huge concept; the **5** is so
important it is called the **DOMINANT** (think Circle of 5ths, the 1-4-5 progression, power
chords spelled 1-5-8). Most chords contain 1-5-8 and most scales are built around them.

### 1-5-8 must be known 4 ways (THINK · READ · HEAR · SPEAK)

- **THINK** — you know some concepts about the pattern (more to come).
- **READ** — you'll learn to recognize it on paper (next lessons on notation).
- **HEAR** — to ear-train it, think of a famous piece that features it: **1-5-8 is "Also
  Sprach Zarathustra" by R. Strauss** (theme from the movie _2001_).
- **SPEAK** — its **finger pattern** is the same shape as a **POWER CHORD**. That same
  combination of notes shows up inside many other common finger patterns (e.g. the major
  scale, the E-Form major chord, the A-Form minor chord — diagrams highlight the 1, 5 and 8
  inside each).

## Lesson 9 — Notation

Music is a language, so you must learn to **READ** it. Notation is one of the 6 Main Areas
and one of the 4 Ways to Know your pitch patterns. It seems intimidating only because it's
usually explained poorly — you learned to THINK and SPEAK a language long before you learned
to READ it.

**WRITTEN MUSIC IS A GRAPH** with 2 axes:

- **Vertical axis = PITCH** (higher on the graph = higher pitch).
- **Horizontal axis = TIME** (left to right = progressing through time = RHYTHM).

There are symbols for each axis, plus symbols for other areas (dynamics, technique).

### Pitch notation — the Grand Staff

A piano has 88 notes (~7 octaves). Notating that whole range would need ~26 lines and spaces
— too "busy". So the very highest and lowest notes were dropped (~3 octaves' worth), leaving
~11 lines and spaces, which were then split into two 5-line staves with an "invisible" line
between them. The note on that invisible line is the most famous of all: **MIDDLE C.** The
**TREBLE (G) CLEF** and **BASS (F) CLEF** tell you which range you're in; together they form
the **GRAND STAFF.** Notes higher or lower than the staff sit on partial lines called
**LEDGER LINES.**

The grand staff comfortably notates ~4 octaves (from 2 ledger lines below the bass clef up
to 2 above the treble clef) — about the guitar's total range. The same note can sometimes be
written in two different places.

### Guitar notation quirk

- The open strings' **actual pitches** would be notated across both clefs of the grand staff.
- But **GUITAR NOTATION is written one octave HIGHER than it actually sounds**, so it fits
  neatly on the G (treble) clef alone, using ledger lines below for the low strings.
- Notes **stacked vertically** are played simultaneously — that's how CHORDS appear on the
  staff.

### Tablature (TAB)

A guitar-specific notation many find easier to read, but it conveys less information:

- **TAB has 6 lines, one per string.** Numbers on a line indicate which **FRET** to play on
  that string.
- TAB tells you what to do with your fingers, but **not** what notes you're playing or the
  RHYTHM — it's hard to learn an unfamiliar song from TAB alone.
- Extra symbols indicate ARTICULATIONS: **H = hammer-on, P = pull-off, S = slide, B = bend,
  R = release, PB = pre-bend, X = muted note, PM = palm mute, 〜 = vibrato** (covered fully
  in later lessons). Fuller symbol lists appear in guitar magazines.

## Lesson 10 — More About Notation: Key Signatures & Rhythm Notation

### Key Signatures

There are **12 MAJOR KEYS** (one per note). In any piece, one of the 12 notes is the NUCLEUS
NOTE; that tells you the key. Each key has a **KEY SIGNATURE** — the set of SHARPS or FLATS
you must play to execute a Major Diatonic Scale starting on that key's note. Because the
major scale is the central pattern, "keyness"/tonality is based on it.

- Example: in **G Major**, to keep the W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern the 7th note must be F# (not F,
  and not Gb — that would put two kinds of G in the scale). So the key of **G = 1 sharp
  (F#)**. A single sharp is placed on the top line of the G clef; every F is then played as
  F# unless a **NATURAL sign (♮)** cancels it.
- Some keys use flats: e.g. **Db Major = 5 flats.**
- Key signatures avoid alphabetic redundancy within a key.
- Notes sharped/flatted **outside** the key signature are called **ACCIDENTALS.** There are
  also **double sharps (𝄪)** and **double flats (♭♭).**
- **Enharmonic keys** are spelled two ways (e.g. B = Cb, F# = Gb, C# = Db), which is why
  some keys carry 6 or 7 sharps/flats even though there are only 5 black keys.
- Every major key has a **RELATIVE MINOR** key rooted on its 6th degree (e.g. C major / A
  minor, both 0 sharps/flats). A full chart lists all 12 major keys with their relative
  minors and enharmonic spellings.

### Rhythm Notation & Time Signatures

The horizontal axis of the graph is TIME: symbols tell you WHEN to play a note and HOW LONG
to hold it. These symbols mean different things depending on the TIME SIGNATURE.

A staff begins with a CLEF, then the KEY SIGNATURE, then the **TIME SIGNATURE** (two stacked
numbers):

- **TOP number = beats per measure** (how many basic beats between bar lines).
- **BOTTOM number = which note value counts as one beat** (4 = quarter note, 2 = half note,
  8 = eighth note).
- The beat values of all notes/rests in a measure add up to the top number.

Common time signatures:

| Symbol                    | Meaning                           |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| **C (Common Time) = 4/4** | 4 beats/measure, quarter = 1 beat |
| **¢ (Cut Time) = 2/2**    | half note = 1 beat                |
| **3/4 (Waltz Time)**      | —                                 |
| **6/8**                   | an eighth note = 1 beat           |

Time signatures with a top number of 5, 7, 9, 11, etc. are rare.

**Note values (how many beats each lasts) depending on the time signature:**

| Note               | in 4/4 | in 2/2 | in 6/8 |
| ------------------ | ------ | ------ | ------ |
| **Whole note**     | 4      | 2      | 8      |
| **Half note**      | 2      | 1      | 4      |
| **Quarter note**   | 1      | 1/2    | 2      |
| **Eighth note**    | 1/2    | 1/4    | 1      |
| **Sixteenth note** | 1/4    | 1/8    | 1/2    |

(There are also 32nd notes; the bottom number of a time signature can also be 16 or 32.)

- A **DOT** in front of a note or rest adds half its value again (a dotted half = 3 beats in
  4/4).
- Two notes of the same pitch **TIED** together equal one note with their combined value.
- Each note value has a matching **REST** (a silence of the same duration).
